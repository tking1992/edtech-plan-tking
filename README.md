# Educational Technology Plan

The purpose of this Educational Technology Plan is to design a practical and inclusive technology plan for a middle school Digital Literacy & Computer Science course. The plan identifies the hardware, software, network infrastructure, and classroom technologies needed to support student learning. It also explains how these tools will be used to teach computer science concepts, support digital literacy, provide feedback, and create meaningful learning activities. In addition, the plan considers evidence-based learning practices, student access, inclusion, ethical and legal responsibilities, and possible challenges such as limited devices, internet issues, or changes to online or hybrid instruction.

## Context

### Course

This course is designed to help students become confident, responsible, and creative users of technology. Students will learn digital citizenship, as well as foundational computer science concepts, including problem-solving, coding, and online safety. Through hands-on projects and collaborative activities, students will use technology to create, communicate, think critically, and better understand how computers and digital tools shape the world around them.

Digital Literacy & Computer Science will be taken by all 7th and 8th grade students. Once in 7th grade and once in 8th.

### Situation

The course addressed in this Educational Technology Plan is a middle school course titled "Computer Science & Digital Literacy." The course is designed for 7th and 8th grade students and will be taken twice (once in 7th grade and once in 8th grade). Because of this aspect, the course needs to be flexible enough to support and challengfe students of varying levels of experience. Some students will be focused on learning basic computer science concepts for the first time, while others will have already been introduced to to coding, digital-citizenship, or other technology-based projects. With this in mind, the course should include foundational skills, as well as offer opportunities for extension, creativity, and deeper problem-solving.

The school context strongly influences the technology decisions in this plan. Assuming this course will be at the site I currently work at, students will likely rely on school-issued Chromebooks to access assignments, coding platforms, digital lessons, and online resources. This is an important asset because it allows the course to use web-based tools such as Google Classroom, Code.org, Google Workspace, and other browser-accessible resources without requiring expensive software installation. The use of Chromebooks also makes it easier for students to access the same materials across different classrooms and learning environments. However, it will also be limited in that same regard as the hardware being used will not be able to run more memory demanding programs/applications. Another limitation for the class will also stem from the fact that students will only be enrolled in the course for one trimester of the academic year before switching to another elective. 

In addition to the aforementioned limitations, other limitations may include students entering with different academic skills, reading levels, technology experience, and confidence with computer science. Some students may be comfortable experimenting with technology, while others may need step-by-step guidance, repeated modeling, and additional support. This means lessons should be chunked into smaller steps and should include visual examples, guided practice, sentence frames, and opportunities for peer collaboration.

Technical constraints also need to be considered. School devices will have limited processing power, storage, software capabilities. With this in mind, we should prioritize cloud-based and browser-based tools. Internet reliability is also important because many coding platforms, videos, and classroom resources require a stable connection. If the network is slow or unavailable, students may not be able to access assignments or complete online tasks. For this reason, we should also include backup options such as pen and paper assignments, printed planning sheets, offline code practice, and flexible deadlines when technical issues occur.

The demographic context of the class also matters. At my school site, the student population is about 47% female and 53% male, with a diverse ethnic makeup that includes a majority Hispanic/Latino population at 68.9%, along with White students, Asian students, African American students, Pacific Islander students, Filipino students, American Indian/Alaskan Native students, and students of two or more races. The school data also shows that about 45% of students are at or above grade level, while about 55% do not meet grade-level readiness and may need interventions.   Because of this, a middle school computer science course should be designed to support students with a wide range of learning needs, language abilities, prior knowledge, and confidence with technology. Since some students may not have reliable internet or a personal device at home, the course should provide meaningful class time to complete digital work using school resources. The plan should also include inclusive practices such as captions on videos, visual directions, accessible document formats, vocabulary support, collaborative learning structures, and project choices that allow students to connect computer science to their own cultures, interests, communities, and future career possibilities. 

Overall, the assets of the course include access to school technology, online learning platforms, classroom display tools, and opportunities for hands-on digital projects. The main constraints include mixed grade levels, varied student experience, possible device and internet limitations, and the need to make computer science accessible to all learners. These factors shape the plan by requiring technology choices that are affordable, browser-based, flexible, inclusive, and directly connected to student learning.

## Plan

### Software Specifications

Students in the Digital Literacy & Computer Science course will use Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebook computers as their primary classroom devices. The Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebooks provide students with consistent access to digital assignments, coding activities, online tutorials, collaborative documents, and teacher feedback. Teachers have access to will have access to BenQ Smartboards, Aeries, Google Classroom, Neptune Navigate and will likely need to utilize the free curriculum on Code.org to help student learning. Devices at the school site are administerated by tech services and as such any software and other online services will need to be vetted by that team for approval before moving forward with any other software outside of these already approved applications/services.

### Hardware Specifications

The hardware specifications for the Digital Literacy & Computer Science course are based on the technology already available to students and staff. The class is already limited by existing student devices, school resources, and administrative approval processes. Therefore, this plan does not depend on, or plan on, purchasing new or specialized equipment. Instead, the course is designed around hardware that is already available and realistic for daily classroom use.

Students will use Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebook computers as their primary classroom devices. Each student will need access to a functioning Chromebook with a working screen, keyboard, trackpad, charging port, and battery. Since students will complete technology-based assignments during class, the devices must be charged or have access to charging when needed. The Chromebook is the most important piece of student hardware because it provides each student with the means for participating in lessons, completing activities, and submitting assignments.

The classroom will also need reliable access to charging equipment. This may include student chargers, classroom charging stations, or access to outlets when devices are low on battery. Because the course depends on regular student device use, charging access is an important hardware requirement. If students come to class with uncharged devices or damaged chargers, it could limit their ability to participate in the lesson.

Existing teacher hardware includes a teacher computer and a BenQ Smartboard. The teacher computer will be used to manage classroom instruction and assignments while the Smartboard will be used to display materials, demonstrate tasks, and support student learning. 

Additional classroom hardware may include speakers, a document camera, and headphones or earbuds if available. Speakers may be useful when the teacher plays instructional videos or demonstrates multimedia content for the class. A document camera can support instruction by allowing the teacher to display printed planning sheets, diagrams, or handwritten examples. Headphones or earbuds may support students when they need to listen to individual instructions, videos, or accessibility tools, but they should be considered supportive hardware rather than a required purchase unless already provided by the school.

A major constraint of the hardware plan is that the class must work within the equipment already assigned to students and teachers. The plan should not rely on specialized hardware such as robotics kits, desktop computers, tablets, 3D printers, or external monitors unless those items are already available and approved for classroom use. While those tools could expand student learning opportunities, they may require additional funding, maintenance, storage, setup time, and administrative approval. For this reason, the required hardware for the course should remain practical and limited to what the school can reliably provide.

Overall, the required hardware for the Digital Literacy & Computer Science course includes student Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebooks, access to chargers or charging stations, a teacher computer, and a BenQ Smartboard. Optional supportive hardware may include headphones, speakers, a document camera, spare chargers, and backup devices when available. This hardware plan is realistic because it is based on existing classroom resources and does not depend on equipment that may be difficult to purchase, approve, maintain, or distribute equitably.

### Application

The technologies in this plan will be used to support a one-trimester Digital Literacy & Computer Science course for combined 7th and 8th grade students. Since students will only have the course for one trimester, the technology plan needs to be focused, realistic, and structured around tools that are already available to students and teachers. Students will use Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebooks, while the instructor will use tools such as the BenQ Smartboard, Google Classroom, Aeries, and Code.org. These technologies will be used for in-class instruction, assignments, student practice, homework when appropriate, feedback, and assessment.

Code.org will serve as the main instructional platform for the course. Since the class is limited to one trimester, Code.org’s Express Course 2025 is a practical choice because it is designed as an eight-week course with 31 lessons and is intended for students in grades 6–8. The course schedule provides approximately 215 instructional minutes per week, which gives students time to complete lessons, practice coding skills, receive teacher support, and reflect on their learning. Because the Express Course is already structured, it increases efficiency by giving the teacher an organized sequence of lessons instead of requiring every activity to be created from scratch.

Neptune Navigate is a digital course approved by the school district that teaches digital citizenship. It provides comprehensive online lessons customized for students and staff, making it easy to teach responsible Digital Citizenship, in the classroom. Since the school already has the program, the Digital Literacy & Computer Science teacher can utilize Neptune Navigate to teach digital citizenship to students. This also aligns with the districts desire to teach students responsible digital citizenship and also alleviates the burden of modifying schedules for every teacher to teach it on an assigned day. 

In class, the teacher will use the BenQ Smartboard to introduce lessons, model coding concepts, demonstrate how to use Code.org, and walk students through problem-solving strategies. For example, the teacher may display a coding puzzle, model how to identify the goal, show how to test a solution, and demonstrate how to debug errors. This supports effective instruction because students can see the thinking process before they are asked to work independently. It also supports evidence-based pedagogy because lessons can be broken into smaller steps, important information can be visually highlighted, and students can move from teacher modeling to guided practice and then independent work.

Students will use their Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebooks during class to access Google Classroom, complete Code.org lessons, respond to reflection questions, and submit assignments. Most of the work will be completed during class because students may have different levels of access to technology or internet at home. Class time will be used for direct instruction, guided practice, independent coding practice, collaborative problem-solving, and short written reflections. This makes the class more inclusive because students are not expected to rely heavily on home technology access in order to succeed.

Google Classroom will be used as the central location for course organization. The teacher will post lesson directions, links to Code.org activities, reflection questions, project instructions, rubrics, reminders, and makeup work. Students will use Google Classroom to find assignments, submit written responses, track due dates, and review teacher feedback. This improves efficiency because students have one consistent location for class materials. It also supports inclusion because students who are absent, need extra time, or need to reread directions can return to the posted materials whenever needed.

Assignments will include Code.org lessons, short coding challenges, digital citizenship activities, written reflections, and small projects. Students may complete coding puzzles that focus on sequencing, loops, conditionals, events, debugging, and problem-solving. To show understanding, students may also write short explanations of what their code does, describe how they solved a problem, or reflect on a mistake they corrected. These assignments are effective because they require students to do more than complete a digital task; they also need to explain their thinking and demonstrate understanding.

Homework will be limited and used only when it supports learning in a realistic way. Since some students may not have reliable internet or a personal device at home, the course should prioritize in-class completion of technology-based assignments. When homework is assigned, it may include short reflection questions, vocabulary review, project planning, or reviewing posted instructions in Google Classroom. If online homework is necessary, students should have class time to begin the work and flexible support if home access is a barrier.

Since students will take this course a second time in 8th grade, the plan also needs to account for repeat enrollment. Students taking the course for the first time can complete the Code.org Express Course as their foundation in 7th grade. Students taking the course a second time in 8th grade may need a different learning pathway so they are not simply repeating the same assignments. Another option would be to place returning 8th grade students in a mentorship role where they support 7th grade students during guided practice, help peers troubleshoot problems, and model positive collaboration. Another option is to select a second Code.org course from the curriculum catalog that is better suited for returning students and allows them to continue developing their coding and problem-solving skills at a higher level.

The mentorship option supports inclusive pedagogy because it allows more experienced students to build confidence and leadership while giving newer students peer support. However, mentorship should not replace learning for returning students. If 8th grade students mentor others, they should still complete their own extension tasks, reflections, or advanced coding challenges. A second-course option may be more appropriate if the goal is to provide returning students with a clearly different curriculum and continued academic growth.

The use of these technologies is justified by efficiency, effectiveness, evidence-based learning, and inclusion. Code.org provides a structured course sequence that fits the trimester timeline, Google Classroom keeps materials organized, the BenQ Smartboard supports whole-class modeling, Chromebooks provide consistent student access, and Aeries supports grade reporting and progress monitoring. The plan is effective because students receive direct instruction, guided practice, independent practice, feedback, and opportunities to explain their thinking. It reflects evidence-based pedagogy by using modeling, chunked instruction, visual examples, repeated practice, feedback, and reflection. It also supports inclusion by keeping most technology-dependent work in class, providing clear digital directions, allowing students to work at different paces, and creating options for students with different levels of prior experience.

Overall, the technologies will be used to create a structured, practical, and accessible computer science learning environment. The course will use Code.org Express Course as the main pathway for first-time students while also planning for the needs of returning 8th grade students. This approach allows the course to fit within the limits of a trimester schedule while still supporting meaningful learning, student engagement, and continued growth in digital literacy and computer science.

### Impacts

The technology will help all students learn by giving them consistent access to computer science content, coding practice, digital literacy activities, and teacher feedback through their Lenovo 300E Gen 3 Chromebooks. Since this course does not appear to rely on a traditional textbook, students will access most class materials through Google Classroom and Code.org. Google Classroom will provide one organized location for assignments, directions, links, rubrics, and feedback, while Code.org’s Express Course 2025 will provide a structured sequence of computer science lessons appropriate for middle school students. This supports students who need repeated access to instructions, students who are absent, and students who benefit from visual and step-by-step learning.

The use of classroom technology also supports inclusive instruction because students will complete most digital work during class time rather than being expected to rely on home internet or personal devices. The teacher can use the BenQ Smartboard to model coding activities, demonstrate problem-solving strategies, and walk students through assignments before they work independently on their Chromebooks. This helps students with different levels of experience, including beginners, students who need extra support, and students who may be taking the course for a second time.
